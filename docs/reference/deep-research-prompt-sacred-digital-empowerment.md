## Prompt

RESEARCH REQUEST: Autonomous AI Agent Platforms for Sacred and Mission-Driven Organizations — Digital Empowerment Without Compromise

CONTEXT: Self-Realization Fellowship (SRF, founded 1920 by Paramahansa Yogananda) and Yogoda Satsanga Society of India (YSS, founded 1917) are building an AI agent platform to empower non-technical staff to create digital applications autonomously.

The platform's users are:
- Monastics who create teaching aids, meditation resources, and retreat materials. Their primary expertise is contemplative practice, not technology.
- Administrative staff who build event sites (annual Convocation, summer retreats, youth camps) and internal tools. They understand organizational needs but not software engineering.
- Center leaders worldwide who create local community pages and event announcements. They vary widely in technical comfort.
- YSS partners in India who build sites with Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, and Kannada language support. They serve seekers on low-end devices with intermittent connectivity.
- An executive and philanthropist who views cross-organization dashboards, approves major promotions, and tracks costs.

A staff member enters a prompt, a team of AI agents builds a complete application (research → design → build → validate → deploy), and the result is delivered via email with a live URL. The staff member reviews the site, leaves feedback through an overlay, agents revise, and eventually the staff member promotes (manual approval required) or declines (preserved as institutional record).

Non-negotiable constraints that make this fundamentally different from a typical AI platform:

VERBATIM FIDELITY (PRI-01): The AI generates user interfaces and code but NEVER generates, paraphrases, summarizes, or synthesizes the spiritual content it displays. Yogananda's published words must appear exactly as published — not one word changed. The AI is a librarian who finds and presents; it is never an oracle who interprets or creates. AI generation, synthesis, or cloning of any media representing Yogananda or the lineage gurus is prohibited — text, voice, image, or video.

DELTA COMPLIANCE (PRI-09): No user identification, no session tracking, no behavioral profiling. The platform audits what AI agents do (for institutional learning) but never audits what seekers do (for privacy). Curation algorithms derive intelligence from the corpus itself, never from user behavior — even anonymized.

CALM TECHNOLOGY (PRI-08): No push notifications, no autoplay, no engagement tracking, no gamification, no reading streaks, no urgency theater. The portal waits; it does not interrupt. Notifications are digest-mode by default. Quiet hours respected. Technology requires the smallest possible amount of attention.

GLOBAL EQUITY (PRI-05): A seeker in rural Bihar on a JioPhone with 2G connectivity and a seeker in Los Angeles on fiber both receive the complete experience. JavaScript under 100KB. First Contentful Paint under 1.5 seconds on 3G. Progressive enhancement: HTML is the foundation. No feature gating behind connectivity.

10-YEAR HORIZON (PRI-10): Philanthropically funded with a decade design horizon. The platform must outlive any single vendor, framework, or AI model generation. Standard protocols (SQL, HTTP, Git) at every boundary. Business logic has zero framework imports.

For each topic below, find the closest precedent — even if imperfect — and identify what is genuinely unprecedented. We need to understand where we're building on known ground and where we're walking into uncharted territory. Emphasize edge cases and failure modes: when we are building this, what will we wish we had designed for? When we are operating it, what will we wish we had asked about? Where are the failure cases? What haven't we considered?

Conduct a thorough investigation across the following 6 topics. For each, cite specific implementations, papers, or documented experience from 2024–2026.

Survey the intersection of autonomous AI systems and organizations with sacred content obligations. Not chatbots, not search tools — autonomous systems that BUILD things.

Catholic Church digital operations, megachurch networks (Church of the Highlands, Hillsong, Saddleback), Buddhist digital projects (CBETA, BuddhistDoor, Plum Village), Islamic institutions (Quran.com, Muslim Pro), Jewish organizations (Sefaria, Chabad.org), Hindu organizations (ISKCON/Vedabase, Art of Living, Chinmaya Mission). Any implementations? How is the prohibition enforced technically — not just by policy but by system design?
- Content fidelity verification: given a passage displayed on an AI-built page, automatically verify it is VERBATIM from the source corpus. Not plagiarism detection (which measures similarity) but fidelity verification (which demands identity). What tools, approaches, or algorithms exist? What accuracy rates? What about fidelity across languages — verifying a Hindi translation matches the approved Hindi edition, not just any Hindi rendering?
- How do organizations with authoritative content canons (religious texts, legal codes, medical protocols, regulatory standards) prevent AI from subtly distorting the content through paraphrase, reordering, selective omission, or context stripping? What safeguards exist beyond "tell the AI not to"?
- The attribution chain: every displayed passage must carry full provenance (author, book, chapter, page number). In an AI-built site, how do you ensure the attribution chain remains intact from source database through agent-generated code to rendered HTML? What breaks the chain? What about passages that span page boundaries?
- Non-profit digital transformation patterns applicable to religious organizations. How have large non-profits (Red Cross, UNICEF, Gates Foundation) used AI for internal capability building? What adoption patterns transfer to religious contexts and what doesn't?
- What are we not considering? The theological dimension: does AI-mediated creation carry different weight than human creation in sacred contexts? When AI builds a page displaying Yogananda's words and a monastic approves it, is the spiritual integrity of the presentation maintained? TOPIC 2: Empowering Non-Technical Contemplative Communities with Autonomous AI

The platform's users are not software engineers. They are monastics, administrators, and community leaders whose primary mental models are contemplative, organizational, and relational — not computational. The concept of autonomous AI agents building software is genuinely unprecedented in their experience.

Investigate:
- Historical patterns of monastic and contemplative communities adopting technology. How have monastic communities (Benedictine, Buddhist, Hindu ashrams) historically adopted printing, telephony, computing, and the internet? What adoption patterns predict success or resistance? What role does institutional authority play?
- The "prompt coaching" role: IT staff helping non-engineers express their needs to AI systems. Is this a documented pattern in any organization? How do organizations build this capability? Is there a training methodology for teaching non-technical people to write effective prompts for autonomous systems — not chatbot prompts, but prompts that specify complete applications?
- Progressive disclosure in AI orchestration interfaces: simple for basic use (pick a template, edit the prompt, click Run), powerful for advanced use (custom workflow DAGs, model selection, validation gate configuration). Examples of AI interfaces designed for audiences ranging from zero technical literacy to moderate technical comfort? What works?
- How do non-engineers evaluate AI-generated websites? When a monastic reviews an AI-built retreat registration page, they can assess "does this feel right?" and "is the information correct?" but not "is the code well-structured?" or "will this scale?" What review interfaces help non-technical reviewers provide effective feedback? Visual diff tools? Side-by-side before/after? Inline commenting overlays? Guided review checklists?
- The "experiment sandbox" pattern: zero-consequence environments where staff learn by doing without fear of breaking anything or wasting money. How do organizations create psychological safety for AI experimentation? Any documented patterns from educational technology, simulation-based training, or organizational learning?
- Quality variance: different staff members will produce different-quality prompts. A detailed prompt with acceptance criteria produces a better outcome than "make a nice website." How do organizations handle this variance without creating gatekeeping? Template libraries? AI-assisted prompt improvement (the AI helps the user write a better prompt before executing)? Peer prompt review?
- Cognitive accessibility beyond WCAG: designing for people whose mental models are contemplative rather than computational. A monastic thinks in terms of spiritual intention, service to seekers, and alignment with the guru's vision — not features, components, and APIs. Is there UX research on designing technology interfaces for non-computational thinkers? Interfaces for artists, therapists, clergy, or other professionals whose expertise is relational/intuitive rather than systematic/technical?
- What failure modes will surprise us? Staff who become dependent on the platform and lose existing capabilities. Staff who distrust AI output and refuse to adopt, creating organizational division. The "uncanny valley" of AI-generated sites — good enough to use but subtly wrong in ways that undermine the sacred content's impact. Staff overwhelmed by the VOLUME of AI output (too many options, too many experiments, decision fatigue). The monastic who approves a site without really reviewing it because the review interface didn't surface the right questions.

TOPIC 3: Calm Technology Principles Applied to Autonomous AI Systems

The platform runs AI agent pipelines autonomously — research completes, design completes, build deploys, validation passes. Each event is an opportunity to notify the staff member. The danger: the platform becomes yet another source of digital noise in a contemplative community.

Investigate:
- Mark Weiser, John Seely Brown, and Amber Case's calm technology principles applied specifically to autonomous AI notifications. "Technology should require the smallest possible amount of attention." "Technology should inform and create calm." How do these principles translate to concrete notification design for multi-stage AI workflows?
- Digest vs. real-time notifications for autonomous processes: evidence on which approach preserves attention while maintaining awareness. If a 7-stage pipeline takes 15 minutes, should the user get 7 notifications, 1 digest, or just the final "it's ready" notification? What does the research on interruption and attention say?
- Quiet hours and schedule-aware batching: implementations where AI systems respect contemplative or organizational schedules. Monasteries have prayer schedules. Ashrams have meditation periods. Business offices have end-of-day. How do notification systems accommodate these rhythms? Not just "do not disturb" but intelligent batching — accumulate notifications during quiet hours, deliver a calm summary when the person is available.
- The anti-pattern: AI systems that generate urgency. "Your experiment is ready — review now!" "3 experiments awaiting your approval!" "Build failed — action required!" How do organizations resist the pull toward urgency-driven notification design? What does a truly calm notification look like for an AI agent pipeline?
- Multi-channel notification orchestration: email, Microsoft Teams, SMS, and in-app dashboard. Priority routing where routine updates go to email digest, important milestones go to Teams, and only genuine emergencies (budget exceeded, security vulnerability found) go to SMS. Examples of notification systems that implement this kind of channel-priority routing? What goes wrong — notifications sent to the wrong channel, duplicate notifications across channels, notification storms during a batch of experiments?
- Anonymous organizational awareness without surveillance: showing staff that the platform is active and valuable ("12 experiments completed this month, 8 promoted to production") without revealing who created what. Patterns for organizational visibility that respect privacy? The resonance counter pattern — collective awareness without individual identification.
- What failure modes will we discover in production? The notification that was technically "calm" but delivered at 11pm because the timezone wasn't configured correctly. The digest that batched 15 notifications into an overwhelming email. The staff member who missed a critical validation failure because it was buried in a digest. The experiment that ran to completion and incurred costs because the staff member never saw the "research complete — review before continuing?" notification. The quiet hours configuration that accidentally blocked urgent security alerts.

TOPIC 4: Privacy-Preserving AI Operations at Organizational Scale

DELTA compliance means: no user identification, no session tracking, no behavioral profiling. But the platform needs to track WHAT IT DOES — costs, agent sessions, validation results, experiment outcomes — for institutional learning. This creates a fundamental tension between operational transparency and user privacy.

Investigate:
- The audit boundary: auditing AI agent behavior (what the system did) vs. auditing user behavior (what people did). How do organizations draw this line technically? Where does "the system processed a request" end and "a user made a request" begin? Is the staff member who created an experiment a "user" whose identity should be tracked, or an "operator" whose actions are part of the system audit trail?
- Observability instrument configuration for privacy: Sentry and New Relic in AI-generated applications. SPECIFIC configuration patterns that capture system behavior (error rates, response times, Apdex scores, JavaScript errors, API latency) WITHOUT capturing user behavior (sessions, interaction patterns, page flows, click paths). Sentry's session replay must be disabled. New Relic's session trace must be disabled. What other data collection features in these tools leak user behavior by default and must be explicitly disabled?
- Cost attribution without user tracking: "The Convocation 2027 experiment cost $4.20" is necessary operational data. "Staff member X has spent $47 this month" — is this legitimate operational tracking or privacy-violating surveillance? How do organizations with strong privacy commitments handle cost attribution in multi-user AI platforms? Can you attribute costs to experiments and projects without attributing them to specific people?
- Content-based discovery in AI-generated sites: when AI builds a teachings portal, it must create discovery pathways (related passages, thematic groupings, suggested readings) from the CONTENT ITSELF rather than from user behavior patterns. No collaborative filtering. No "people who read X also read Y." Discovery derived purely from textual similarity, thematic taxonomy, and curated collections. What approaches exist? How do privacy-preserving recommendation systems work? What's the quality gap between content-based and behavior-based recommendation?
- GDPR and privacy-by-design patterns that go BEYOND regulatory minimums — organizations that adopt strong privacy as a spiritual or ethical value, not just a compliance requirement. Apple's privacy stance, Signal's design philosophy, Proton's approach. How do these translate to an AI agent platform? What architectural patterns enforce privacy by default rather than by policy?
- What privacy failures will surprise us in production? An agent that adds Google Fonts (which leaks IP addresses to Google). An agent that includes a third-party analytics script because a tutorial in its training data recommended it. A Sentry breadcrumb configuration that captures URL parameters containing search queries (which could reveal what seekers are searching for). A New Relic custom attribute that captures user-agent strings (which can be used for fingerprinting). An experiment that uses a third-party API that logs request metadata. The debugging session where an engineer examines Sentry error details and inadvertently sees user device information that was supposed to be excluded.

TOPIC 5: 10-Year Platform Architecture in a Decade of AI Model Evolution

The platform is funded with a 10-year horizon. The AI models, frameworks, and even the concept of "agents" may look fundamentally different by 2036. The architecture must survive this evolution.

Investigate:
- The "model as parameter" pattern: same workflow definition, same agent roles, different underlying model. Today it's Claude Opus and Sonnet; in 2028 it might be Claude 6 or a model from a competitor or an open-source model. How do you abstract the model layer so that swapping models requires changing a configuration value, not rewriting the platform? What abstraction layers exist? LiteLLM, portkey.ai, Anthropic's own SDK versioning patterns? How robust are these abstractions in practice?
- MCP (Model Context Protocol) longevity: Anthropic introduced MCP as a standard for tool use. It's gaining adoption (Cursor, Windsurf, IDEs, enterprise tools). But will it survive 10 years? What's the historical precedent for protocol longevity in developer tools? (REST survived 20+ years. SOAP did not. XML-RPC did not. GraphQL has survived 10+ years and is healthy. gRPC is 9 years old and growing.) What attributes predict protocol longevity? Does MCP have them?
- Which components of 2026 AI agent platforms will look obsolete by 2030? Specific agent frameworks (CrewAI, LangGraph) — will they survive, or will they be replaced by native SDK capabilities? Prompt engineering techniques — will they matter when models are much more capable? The concept of "roles" for agents — will future models need role specialization, or will a single powerful model render it unnecessary? Vector databases and embedding approaches — will they be replaced by native long-context models?
- The vendor lock-in assessment for our specific stack: Claude Code SDK (Anthropic), AWS Bedrock (Amazon), Vercel (hosting), Neon PostgreSQL (database), Sentry (error tracking), New Relic (APM). Rate each on a 10-year survival likelihood. Which dependencies are healthiest? Which are riskiest? For each risky dependency, what's the migration path?
- Database-centric vs. API-centric vs. event-centric orchestration: our design centers on MCP as the universal data surface with Neon PostgreSQL as the persistence layer. The alternative: event sourcing (every state change is an event in a log), or API-first (everything is a REST endpoint). Which architectural pattern has the best track record for surviving a decade of technology change?
- Multi-organization shared infrastructure: SRF (US) and YSS (India) share the platform but have independent content corpora, design tokens (Navy/Gold/Cream for SRF, Terracotta/Clay for YSS), approval workflows, language priorities, and potentially different regulatory environments. Patterns for multi-tenant AI platforms that serve organizations with shared mission but independent operations? What breaks — shared models that optimize for one org's patterns at the expense of the other? Shared infrastructure costs that are hard to allocate fairly? Configuration drift between organizations?
- What architectural assumptions will we regret? Building on a single cloud provider (AWS) when multi-cloud might be necessary. Assuming MCP will be the standard when a competitor protocol might win. Assuming agent frameworks are necessary when native model capabilities might make them obsolete. Assuming 30+ specialized roles when future models might not need specialization. Assuming GitHub repositories are the right audit trail when a purpose-built system might be better. Assuming human review remains necessary when AI self-validation might become trustworthy.

TOPIC 6: Human Oversight of Autonomous AI in Sacred Contexts

When AI agents autonomously build sites that present Paramahansa Yogananda's teachings, the governance stakes are higher than typical software delivery. A bug in a commerce site costs money. A distortion in a teachings site costs spiritual integrity.

Investigate:
- The human review bottleneck: at 10 experiments/month, a single IT reviewer can evaluate each carefully. At 100/month, review becomes either a bottleneck (experiments wait days for approval) or a rubber stamp (reviewer clicks "approve" without meaningful evaluation). How do organizations scale human oversight of autonomous AI without sacrificing review quality? Tiered review (AI validates routine checks, humans focus on judgment calls)? Review delegation (different staff review different experiment types)? AI-assisted review (an AI summarizes what changed and flags areas needing human attention)?
- Trust graduation for AI agents: starting with maximum oversight (human reviews everything) and gradually reducing oversight as confidence builds. How do organizations formalize this? What triggers trust ESCALATION (something went wrong, tighten oversight)? What triggers trust RELAXATION (100 experiments with no issues, relax oversight for certain experiment types)? Is there a formal framework for agent trust levels — read-only, propose-to-queue, direct-action-with-audit, full-autonomy?
- AI mission drift: the gradual, unintentional erosion of constraints over many iterations. A content integrity agent that starts tolerating minor variations ("close enough to verbatim"). A design agent that gradually drifts from the design system tokens. A compliance agent that starts treating borderline cases as acceptable. How do you detect mission drift in autonomous AI systems? What monitoring patterns catch slow degradation that no single iteration makes obvious?
- The institutional memory problem: AI agents make design decisions, staff approve them, the system ships. Six months later, someone asks "why was it built this way?" and nobody can answer from memory. The git audit trail has the data, but is it ACCESSIBLE? Can a non-technical person navigate a git history of agent session transcripts to understand why decisions were made? What tools or patterns make AI decision history queryable by humans?
- The maintenance problem: an AI-built application needs a bug fix. No human wrote the code. The original agent sessions that generated the code may not be reproducible (different model version, different context). Is this fundamentally different from the existing "the developer who wrote this left the company" problem? Worse? Better? What maintenance strategies exist for AI-generated systems? Can you point a new AI agent at an existing AI-generated codebase and have it make targeted fixes? What goes wrong?
- Cross-organization governance: SRF and YSS share the platform but have different theological review authorities (SRF's monastic review board vs. YSS's organizational leadership), different content approval workflows, and different cultural contexts. How do federated governance models work for shared AI platforms? What happens when one organization's approval standards diverge from the other's?
- The succession question: the platform is designed for a 10-year horizon. The people who design and approve it today may not be the people operating it in 2033. How do organizations ensure that the intent behind AI platform constraints (verbatim fidelity, calm technology, DELTA compliance) survives leadership transitions? Is codifying constraints in system architecture more durable than codifying them in policy documents?
- What governance failures will we discover in production? The experiment that was promoted because the reviewer didn't understand a subtle content integrity issue (a teaching displayed out of context that changes its meaning). The staff member who created 50 experiments to find the "right" one, consuming budget without meaningful review. The YSS experiment that used SRF design tokens because the organization configuration was wrong. The promoted experiment that broke when a shared dependency was updated by a different experiment. The monastic reviewer who always approves because they trust the AI, creating a systematic oversight gap. The security vulnerability that passed all AI validation gates because it was a novel attack pattern not in any training data.
