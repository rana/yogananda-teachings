# FTR-162: Transition-Based Navigation

## Rationale

### Context

Current navigation paradigms serve seekers who know *what* they're looking for: search (FTR-020) for specific queries, themes (FTR-121) for topic browsing, Four Doors (FTR-138) for emotional entry points. But many seekers know only *where they are* and *where they want to be*: "I am afraid and I want to be peaceful." "I am grieving and I want to find acceptance." "I am confused and I want clarity."

No existing FTR addresses navigation organized around *emotional transitions* — the journey between states. FTR-038 classifies cognitive tasks (what the seeker is doing); FTR-138 offers emotional doors (where the seeker enters). Transition navigation addresses the *arc*: from one state through teachings to another.

This maps directly to Yogananda's pedagogical approach. He frequently organized teachings as journeys: from ignorance to wisdom, from restlessness to stillness, from fear to fearlessness. The portal can mirror this structure.

### Decision

Create a navigation paradigm based on curated emotional state pairs, where each pair maps to a sequence of passages forming a teaching arc from the origin state to the destination state.

### Interaction Model

**Entry:** Seeker selects or is offered a transition pair:
```
From: [afraid]  →  To: [peaceful]
From: [grieving] →  To: [accepting]
From: [restless] →  To: [still]
From: [doubting] →  To: [faithful]
```

**Journey:** A curated sequence of 3–7 passages, ordered by editorial judgment to form a natural progression. Each passage carries a brief editorial note (one sentence, max) indicating its role in the arc: "Acknowledging the fear" → "Understanding its root" → "The practice of courage" → "The peace beyond fear."

**Completion:** The final passage in the sequence links to the Practice Bridge (FTR-055) with a practice suggestion relevant to the transition.

### Emotional State Vocabulary

States are drawn from the Vocabulary Bridge (FTR-028) mapping seeker language to Yogananda's terminology:

| Seeker State | Yogananda Vocabulary | Destination State |
|-------------|---------------------|------------------|
| afraid | delusion, maya, ignorance | peaceful, fearless |
| grieving | attachment, earthly consciousness | accepting, soul-awareness |
| restless | rajasic, outward-flowing | still, interiorized |
| doubting | intellectual skepticism | faithful, direct perception |
| angry | reactive emotion, ego | forgiving, even-minded |
| lonely | sense of separation | connected, omnipresence |

State vocabulary is a parameter per FTR-012. Editorial expands the list as content supports new transitions.

### Knowledge Graph Integration

Each transition pair is a first-class edge type in the knowledge graph (FTR-034):

```
TRANSITION_ARC: {from_state} → passage_1 → passage_2 → ... → passage_n → {to_state}
```

This enables graph traversal queries: "What passages bridge fear to peace across all books?"

### Editorial Dependency

Transition arcs are **editorially curated, not algorithmically generated.** Each arc requires:
1. A human reviewer confirming the passage sequence forms a genuine pedagogical progression
2. Theological review that the emotional framing accurately represents Yogananda's approach
3. Brief editorial notes (one sentence per passage) describing the passage's role in the arc

This is heavy curation. Initial launch should target 6–10 high-demand transition pairs, expanding based on seeker search patterns (FTR-032) and editorial capacity.

### Constraints

- **Verbatim fidelity** (PRI-01) — passages are quoted verbatim. Only the editorial notes and transition framing are authored content.
- **No AI generation** — transition arcs are not generated by AI. AI may suggest candidate passages for editorial review, but the sequence and framing are human-curated.
- **No behavioral tracking** — transition selection is not tracked per user. Aggregate transition popularity may be counted per FTR-031 pattern.
- **Signpost, not therapy** — transition navigation is a reading journey, not a therapeutic intervention. The editorial notes never diagnose, prescribe, or promise outcomes.

### Re-evaluate At

Milestone 3b boundary — after Knowledge Graph (FTR-034) and Vocabulary Bridge (FTR-028) are operational. Editorial capacity assessment required.

### Decision Required From

Editorial + theological review: Does organizing teachings by emotional transition accurately serve Yogananda's pedagogical intent? Is this curation or interpretation (FTR-007)?

### Discovery Research Integration (March 2026)

Research from liturgical design, museum curation, and anthology editing (deep-research-gemini-discovery-without-surveillance.md) provides concrete sequencing patterns for transition arcs:

**The Liturgical Energy Curve.** Centuries of homiletic practice confirm a 4-step optimal arc for moving seekers between emotional states:

1. **Acknowledgment** — a passage validating the origin state (the fear, the grief, the restlessness). The seeker must feel recognized before they can move.
2. **Principle** — a passage offering philosophical perspective (the transient nature of material conditions, the larger spiritual context).
3. **Climax** — a catalytic passage instructing on a specific contemplative response (practice, reframing, surrender). The point of highest transformative intensity.
4. **Consolation** — a deeply consoling passage describing the destination state (the peace beyond fear, the acceptance beyond grief). Integration and stillness.

This maps directly to the 3–7 passage range already specified. For 3-passage arcs: Acknowledgment → Climax → Consolation. For 5+: the full curve with bridging passages.

**Sensory and Emotional Bridging.** To transition from state A to state C, locate intermediary passages that contain metadata elements of both. Moving from Fear to Peace: the bridge passage must acknowledge the storm (Fear) while introducing the shelter (Peace). The portal's emotional quality and rasa metadata enable this — query for passages with mixed emotional signatures.

**Anthology Framing Notes.** The one-sentence editorial notes per passage should explicitly illuminate the emotional shift. Not "Here is a related passage" but "Here, Yogananda's voice shifts from acknowledging the weight of fear to revealing the courage already present within..." This is the museum audio guide pattern — the docent whispering context between artworks.

**Bounded sequences prevent fatigue.** Research across museum exhibitions and media consumption confirms that beyond 5–7 highly demanding items, cognitive fatigue sets in without a substantial change in mode. The 3–7 passage limit already specified is research-validated. Prefer 4–5 for emotionally intense transitions.
