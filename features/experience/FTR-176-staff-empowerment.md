# FTR-176: Staff Empowerment and Onboarding

## Rationale

The concept of autonomous AI agents building applications is genuinely new. Non-technical staff (monastics, administrators, center leaders) need guided entry points, examples, and calm notification design to adopt this capability. The platform must help people expand their understanding of what's possible.

## Specification

### Who This Serves

| Audience | What They Can Do |
|----------|-----------------|
| SRF Staff | Build event sites, campaigns, internal tools |
| Monastics | Create teaching aids, meditation resources, retreat materials |
| YSS Partners | Build YSS-branded sites with Indian language support |
| Center Leaders | Create local center pages, event announcements |
| IT Department | Manage platform, create templates, optimize workflows |
| Executive/Philanthropist | View cross-org dashboard, approve promotions, track costs |

### AI-Burnout Paradox (Research-Informed)

Supervising AI output may be MORE cognitively exhausting than building manually (Gemini Prompt 2). The platform could overwhelm contemplative users — monastics and staff whose primary expertise is meditation, not technology review.

**Mitigations:**
- **Promptframes:** Structured templates that constrain AI output into reviewable chunks. Staff review a visual diff of what changed, not raw code. 3.2x more consistent outputs from template-guided prompts vs. freeform.
- **Visual diffs for contemplative review:** Show before/after screenshots, not code diffs. Highlight what's new in the live experimental site. The review interface is designed for people who think in images and experiences, not syntax.
- **Progressive complexity:** Start with simple experiments (content site from provided materials). Graduate to full autonomous builds only after comfort develops.
- **Review load limits:** Platform caps how many experiments can be in "awaiting review" state per person. Prevents accumulation.

### Cognitive Offloading Risk

Frequent AI tool usage correlates with decreased critical thinking abilities (Gerlich 2025, n=666). The platform must actively prevent staff from becoming passive consumers of AI output.

**Mitigations:**
- Experiments require staff to articulate clear acceptance criteria before Build starts (the mandatory design-approval gate, FTR-170)
- Review overlay forces specific feedback — not just "approve/reject" but "what would you change?"
- Periodic "build it yourself" workshops where staff create simple sites manually, maintaining hands-on understanding
- Template library guides prompt quality but doesn't eliminate the need for staff judgment

### Human Reviewer Workflow

The platform's value proposition is empowering non-technical staff. The reviewer experience must be as carefully designed as the agent pipeline. Monastics and staff review experiments at three distinct checkpoints, each with different interfaces:

**1. Design-Approval Gate (FTR-170)**

The reviewer sees a design summary page (not raw artifacts):
- **Visual mockups** — Screenshots or wireframes of key pages. If the Design stage produced component layouts, show them rendered, not as JSON.
- **Plain-language architecture summary** — "This site has 5 pages, uses the SRF design system, stores no user data, and costs an estimated $3-5 to build." Generated by the Stakeholder Communicator agent.
- **Decision points** — Highlighted choices the design agents made that the reviewer should weigh in on. Not a wall of decisions — the top 3-5 that most affect the outcome.
- **Three buttons:** Approve (proceed to Build), Revise (write feedback, return to Design), Cancel (decline with reason preserved).

Feedback is freeform text, not a form. The Design agents interpret it. If interpretation is ambiguous, the platform asks a clarifying question before re-running Design — it does not guess.

**2. Experiment Review (post-deployment)**

The reviewer visits the live experimental URL:
- **Review overlay** (existing platform capability) — floating panel for comments anchored to specific page elements
- **Before/after view** — For feature additions to existing projects, show what changed. Visual diff, not code diff.
- **Guided review checklist** — Tailored to the workflow template. A content site checklist: "Are all passages displaying correctly? Is the navigation intuitive? Does it feel worthy of the teachings?" A technical site checklist: "Does login work? Can you complete the main workflow?"
- **Structured feedback** — Not just "approve/reject" but "What would you change?" (FTR-176 cognitive offloading mitigation). The overlay prompts for specific feedback before showing the approve button.
- **Cost and timeline summary** — What this experiment cost, how long it took, which agents did what (from FTR-174 glass box).

**3. Promotion Decision**

The reviewer sees a promotion summary:
- Validation gate results (pass/fail per gate, in plain language — not raw JSON)
- Adversarial exercise results (if run) as a one-page audit report
- Quality score from Meta-Reviewer (FTR-173) if available
- Total cost with breakdown
- **Two buttons:** Promote to Dev (creates PR), Decline (archive with reason)

For complex templates (auth, payments, databases), the promotion screen requires IT co-approval — the staff reviewer's "Promote" enters a queue for IT review rather than immediately creating a PR.

### Onboarding Materials

1. **Example gallery** — Completed experiments with before/after, cost, timeline
2. **Guided tutorials** — "Build your first site in 10 minutes" walkthrough
3. **Template library** — Pre-populated prompts for common SRF needs (3.2x more consistent outputs than freeform prompts)
4. **Video walkthroughs** — AI-generated video tutorials showing the workflow
5. **Office hours** — IT staff available to help craft prompts and review outputs
6. **Experiment sandbox** — Zero-consequence environment for learning

### Calm Notification Design (PRI-08)

- Digest mode by default (one email per state transition)
- Quiet hours respected (batch, deliver at start of business)
- Threshold notifications (only notify if cost exceeds budget or validation fails)
- Teams/SMS reserved for critical events only

### Auto-Generated Reference Materials (Deferred)

Deferred to Phase 3+. Most auto-generated documentation requires human judgment about what matters — auto-generated docs risk noise that creates false coverage. The accessibility report is the exception (structured, checklistable, machine-verifiable) and is produced by the Accessibility Auditor validation gate (FTR-172) at launch.

**Deferred artifacts (Phase 3+ when quality can be evaluated):**

## Edge Cases

- **Prompt quality variance.** A monastic writes "make a nice website for the retreat" and a staff engineer writes a detailed spec with acceptance criteria. The platform produces radically different quality. The monastic doesn't know their prompt was insufficient until they see the result (after burning $5-20 of compute). Resolution: add a **prompt quality check** before the pipeline starts. A lightweight Haiku-level agent scores the prompt on specificity, completeness, and feasibility. Low-scoring prompts get suggested improvements before execution, not after.
- **Cultural adoption failure.** The platform is built, tutorials exist, but staff don't use it. The concept is too foreign. "Office hours" may not be sufficient for genuinely novel capabilities. Resolution: designate 2-3 "champion users" — staff who are early adopters and can demonstrate success. Peer demonstration is more effective than tutorials. Track adoption metrics: experiments per month, unique staff using the platform, time-to-first-experiment per new user.
- **Video tutorial generation infeasibility.** AI voice + video generation doesn't reliably exist at production quality in March 2026. Deferred to Phase 5+. For launch, use screen recordings with text annotations.
- **Staff creates experiment outside their expertise.** A center leader creates a "membership management system" that requires database design, authentication, and payment processing. The platform can build it, but the center leader can't evaluate whether it's correct or secure. Resolution: workflow templates encode complexity levels. Complex templates (auth, payments, databases) require IT co-review at the design-approval gate, not just staff approval.

## Error Cases

- **Constraint violation from ignorance.** A staff member's prompt says "add analytics to track which pages are most popular." The Design stage implements behavioral tracking. The Compliance gate catches it — but the staff member doesn't understand why their request was rejected. Resolution: when a gate rejects, the notification includes an educational explanation: "Your request for page popularity tracking conflicts with PRI-09 (DELTA compliance). Here's what we can do instead: content-based popularity derived from resonance counters, not behavioral tracking. Would you like to revise your prompt?"
- **Email notification goes to spam.** Experiment state transition emails from AWS SES land in the staff member's spam folder. They think the experiment is stuck. Resolution: use a verified sender domain (`notifications@yogananda.tech`), configure SPF/DKIM/DMARC. Include the ops dashboard link in every email so staff can always check status directly.
- **Experiment sandbox data leak.** A staff member experiments in the sandbox with real content (names, event details) that shouldn't be public. The sandbox domain is accessible to anyone with the URL. Resolution: sandbox experiments are never deployed publicly. They render in a preview mode on the ops dashboard only (authenticated). No external domain assigned.
- **Template library grows unwieldy.** After 100+ templates, staff can't find the right one. Resolution: templates have tags (complexity level, project type, audience). AI-assisted template recommendation: "Based on your description, these 3 templates are most relevant." Unused templates (zero experiments in 6 months) are auto-archived.
